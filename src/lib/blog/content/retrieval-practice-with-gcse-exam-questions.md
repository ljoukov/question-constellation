## Recall is a start

Many students turn retrieval practice into quick definitions. That can help, especially for one-mark recall. Longer GCSE answers need another layer.

A student may remember the word mitochondria and still fail to explain why more mitochondria matter in a cell. The answer needs the chain: mitochondria, respiration, energy release, process supported.

## Exam retrieval should include structure

Question Constellation treats the answer chain as something to retrieve. The student is asking two questions at once: what fact do I know, and what links does this answer need?

That makes retrieval more exam-shaped. The prompt is a real question, the checklist is tied to marks, and the next question asks for transfer rather than a repeat of the same sentence.

## A simple revision routine

Pick one question. Write the answer without notes. Reveal the mark checklist. Rewrite the answer with the missing links. Then open a nearby question from the same constellation.

- Attempt first.
- Check against the chain.
- Rewrite the weak answer.
- Transfer to a related question.

The routine is short, but it avoids the trap of reading a model answer and feeling fluent without proving recall.

## Why this matters near the exam

In the final weeks, many students reread notes because it feels calm. Exam-shaped retrieval is less comfortable, but it gives better information. It shows what the student can produce and where the missing link sits.
